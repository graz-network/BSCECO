## 2. Expert review pass

### A. Macro economist expert

#### Input used
- 2019 work
- bundled economics literature in the archive
- 2026 governance update

#### Retained insights
- The work correctly identified the substitution pressure of capital for labor in a small open economy.
- The emphasis on tax incidence, firm adaptation, and competitiveness remains useful.
- The warning that firms may shift burdens back onto labor still survives.

#### Obsolete assumptions
- The work is too tied to **robots as physical capital**.
- It underestimates the scale effects of software-like AI and general-purpose models.
- It treats automation as more sector-bound than current AI diffusion suggests.

#### Updated framing
In 2026, the key macro problem is less "how many robots replace workers?" and more "how AI changes task composition, wage dispersion, intangible capital, market concentration, and the fiscal base." General-purpose AI does not merely replace labor in factory-like settings. It amplifies scale, speed, and cross-border deployability.

#### Open problems
- How to measure AI-enabled output when AI is embedded in ordinary workflows
- How to track labor-share effects when AI complements some skilled labor but displaces routine cognitive tasks
- How to distinguish productivity gain from rent extraction in AI-heavy markets

#### Recommended extension angle
Move from a robot-tax model toward a **political economy of AI-enabled intangible capital**, focusing on labor share, task restructuring, and the erosion of payroll-linked financing.

---

### B. Fiscality expert

#### Input used
- 2019 work
- bundled tax literature in the archive
- current European and Swiss AI-regulation trajectory

#### Retained insights
- The work was right to doubt the administrative efficiency of a robot tax.
- It correctly flagged localization, definition, and avoidance problems.
- It correctly treated VAT and broader output-side tools as more plausible than taxing physical inputs.

#### Obsolete assumptions
- A robot-specific tax base is even less coherent in 2026 because the relevant productive systems are often software-based, distributed, licensed, or cloud-hosted.
- The taxable-subject problem is now broader than robotics and reaches AI models, downstream integrators, and AI-enhanced firms.

#### Updated framing
The strongest fiscal question in 2026 is not whether to tax robots, but whether existing tax bases still track value creation when production shifts toward software, data, models, and scalable AI services.

#### Open problems
- How to identify the economically relevant taxpayer in AI value chains
- How to preserve social-insurance financing when payroll is no longer the obvious base
- How to avoid double counting when AI is embedded in firm-level assets and services

#### Recommended extension angle
Reject full robot-style taxation. Replace it with a comparative analysis of:
- payroll-base reform
- value-added broadening
- profit-based approaches
- sector-specific levies only where clearly justified

### C. Law doctor

#### Input used
- 2019 work
- bundled Oberson/Floridi-style legal references
- current Swiss, EU, Council of Europe, and OECD governance developments

#### Retained insights
- The work correctly saw that legal status matters once one moves from taxing inputs to attributing output, liability, or income.
- It correctly identified legal personality as the real doctrinal bottleneck.

#### Obsolete assumptions
- The 2019 frame treats legal personality as the obvious frontier of AI regulation.
- By 2026, the main legal trend is not toward granting AI legal personhood, but toward regulating human and corporate actors across the AI lifecycle.

#### Updated framing
A defensible 2026 legal analysis distinguishes between:
1. **full legal personhood**, which remains highly implausible;
2. **functional attribution devices**, which allocate acts, risks, and duties without making AI a person;
3. **limited legal digital personality**, understood as a narrow doctrinal tool for registration, traceability, asset segregation, or responsibility mapping in special contexts.

#### Open problems
- Whether any limited digital-personality concept can improve legal certainty without anthropomorphizing AI
- Whether such a concept would clarify or complicate liability and tax law
- Whether Swiss law would gain more from targeted amendments than from a new subject of law

#### Recommended extension angle
Argue against full AI personhood, but examine whether a **limited functional legal digital personality** could serve as an attribution interface in carefully bounded areas.

## Researcher agent English - independent synwork

### What survives from the bachelor work
The best surviving insight is that the real weakness of robot taxation lies in the mismatch between the tax object and the economic reality of automation. The work already sensed that once automation becomes intangible and quasi-autonomous, taxing the machine itself becomes doctrinally and fiscally unstable.

### What needs revision
The 2019 work must be revised away from a robot-centered ontology. AI in 2026 is not well captured by the image of a machine replacing a worker on a factory floor. The legal and fiscal system increasingly regulates actors around AI systems rather than AI as a self-standing legal person.

### New contribution
The strongest English-language article work is:

> In 2026, the legally and fiscally relevant question is not whether AI should become a full legal person, but whether law needs a limited functional category - a legal digital personality - to organise attribution, accountability, and selected fiscal consequences in AI-mediated environments.

### Emphasis
The English synwork emphasizes institutional design and governance architecture. It treats legal digital personality as a *possible interface tool*, not as an anthropomorphic status.

## Researcher agent French - independent synwork

### Ce qui demeure valable
Le mémoire de 2019 a bien vu que la fiscalité des robots devient doctrinalement fragile dès qu'on quitte la logique du capital matériel et que l'on cherche à attribuer un revenu, une responsabilité ou une capacité contributive à une entité technologique.

### Ce qui doit être révisé
En 2026, le débat ne porte plus prioritairement sur le robot comme objet individualisable, mais sur des systèmes d'IA diffus, logiciels, intégrés et souvent distribués entre plusieurs acteurs. La personnalité juridique pleine et entière reste doctrinalement trop lourde et socialement peu justifiée.

### Nouvelle contribution
La meilleure thèse française est la suivante :

> La personnalité numerique de l'IA ne doit pas etre conçue comme une personnalite juridique generale comparable a celle d'une personne physique ou morale, mais comme une construction fonctionnelle et limitee permettant d'ordonner l'imputation, la transparence et, dans certains cas, les effets fiscaux et assurantiels lies a l'usage de systemes d'IA.

### Accent propre
La synthèse française est plus doctrinale et plus prudente. Elle insiste davantage sur la compatibilité avec le droit suisse et europeen, et sur la nécessité de conserver les centres de responsabilité du côté des personnes physiques et morales.

### Comparison note
The two syntheses converge on the rejection of full AI personhood. They diverge mainly in style:
- the English version foregrounds governance and regulatory architecture;
- the French version foregrounds doctrinal feasibility and legal categorization.

## 4. Analysis

### Shared project question
How should the 2019 robot-tax and electronic-personality debate be reformulated in 2026 once the central object is no longer the robot as a physical machine, but AI as a distributed socio-technical system?

### 2019 baseline
The work argues that robot taxation is mostly inefficient and distortionary. It identifies electronic personality as the only way to move from taxing physical inputs to taxing attributed machine income.

### 2026 reframing
By 2026, the dominant legal movement is actor-based and risk-based governance rather than AI personhood. This does not eliminate the question of legal status; it narrows it. The issue becomes whether a **limited legal digital personality** could improve attribution without creating a full subject of law.

### Macro findings
- The original work still helps explain labor-share pressure and transition costs.
- It is too narrow for software-based AI diffusion.
- The macro debate now turns on task restructuring, scalable intangibles, and concentration.

### Fiscal findings
- A robot tax is less workable in 2026 than in 2019.
- AI pushes the tax question toward firms, value chains, profits, value added, and payroll-base redesign.
- Social-insurance financing remains central, but a machine-specific tax base is weak.

### Legal findings
- Full AI legal personhood remains weakly supported.
- Current law favors obligations on providers, deployers, and operators.
- A narrow legal digital personality may still be worth studying as a technical tool for attribution and governance.

### Integrated conclusion
The project should not be extended into a defense of full AI personhood. Its strongest 2026 contribution is to show why the 2019 intuition about personality mattered, while also explaining why current law has chosen a different path: **regulated responsibility without personhood**. The best article therefore studies whether a *limited legal digital personality* can fill specific attribution gaps without replacing human and corporate responsibility.

## 6. Academic review

### Contribution
The original work contributes a disciplined economic critique of robot taxation and an early bridge toward legal personality.

### Literature position
It is well situated in the late-2010s robot-tax debate but not in the 2024-2026 AI-governance literature.

### Methodological adequacy
The methodology is coherent for its 2019 question, but it is too narrow for 2026 AI systems because it ties the analysis to identifiable robot inputs.

### Clarity of assumptions
Very clear. The small-open-economy and tax-incidence assumptions remain analytically useful.

### Legal and policy robustness
Moderate in 2019; incomplete in 2026. The legal section anticipates the right problem but not the direction of later regulatory developments.

### Originality limits
The work is original as a structured synwork and as an early move toward electronic personality, but much of the robot-tax economics is now mature.

### Revision priorities
1. Reframe the legal object from robots to AI systems.
2. Replace full-personality speculation with a typology of legal-status options.
3. Update the legal backdrop with European and Swiss AI governance.
4. Link tax arguments to actor-based regulation rather than machine-based taxation.

## 7. Originality audit

### What is already original in the work
- It joins tax-incidence analysis with the legal-status problem.
- It identifies electronic personality as the real conceptual hinge for income attribution.

### What is no longer original by 2026
- The general argument that robot taxes are distortionary
- The observation that automation pressures labor income and payroll-linked financing
- The suggestion that electronic personality might someday matter

### Where the new paper can contribute
A 2026 paper can contribute by distinguishing three levels of legal status:
1. no personhood, actor-based regulation only;
2. functional digital personality for attribution in narrow contexts;
3. full personhood, rejected as unjustified.

### Originality risk warnings
- Do not simply restate Oberson-style electronic personality.
- Do not repackage robot-tax skepticism as though it were a new 2026 argument.
- Do not treat AI personhood as inevitable.
